Master Diagnostic Algorithm

Sequential decision tree from referral question to electrodiagnostic conclusion

⚠️ Start with the clinical question — the test is an extension of the clinical examination, not a fishing expedition. The pattern of abnormality must fit the clinical syndrome.
Step 1 — Are the NCS (motor + sensory) NORMAL?
YES — NCS normal
Pathology confined to: muscle (myopathy) · NMJ (MG/LEMS) · very early/mild neuropathy · UMN (NCS always normal in UMN disease) → proceed to needle EMG and/or RNS
NO — NCS abnormal
→ proceed to Step 2
Step 2 — Which modality is affected?
Motor only (↓ CMAP, normal SNAP)
Motor neuropathy · Radiculopathy (prox to DRG) · MND · MMNCB · GBS (early, sensory relatively spared)
Sensory only (↓ SNAP, normal CMAP)
Pure sensory neuropathy · Ganglionopathy (DRG lesion) · Early length-dependent polyneuropathy · Carcinomatous sensory neuropathy
Both (↓ CMAP + ↓ SNAP)
Sensorimotor neuropathy (most common) · → proceed to Step 3
Step 3 — Axonal or Demyelinating? (see Axonal vs Demyelinating section for full criteria)
AXONAL
↓↓ CMAP/SNAP amplitude · CV relatively preserved (may be mildly ↓ from loss of fastest fibers) · Distal latency normal · No conduction block · Needle EMG: denervation → proceed to Step 4
DEMYELINATING
↓↓ CV · ↑↑ Distal latency · Temporal dispersion · ± Conduction block · F-waves absent/prolonged · → proceed to Step 4, then ask: uniform vs non-uniform?
Step 4 — What is the distribution?
Length-dependent
Worse distally · Sural → peroneal → tibial → upper limb (in that order) · Typical: DSPN, CMT, chronic axonal polyneuropathy · Etiology: metabolic, toxic, hereditary
Non-length-dependent
Asymmetric or proximal-predominant · Think: vasculitic, CIDP, plexopathy, ganglionopathy, GBS · Proximal and distal affected similarly
Focal / Multifocal
Mononeuropathy: compressive (CTS, ulnar at elbow) · Multiple mononeuropathies: vasculitic, MMNCB, hereditary neuropathy with pressure palsies (HNPP)
Step 5 — Needle EMG: integrate the picture
Active denervation (fibs, PSWs)
Axonal loss (neuropathic) · Denervation timeline: paraspinals 7–10d → distal limb 2–4wks · Also in inflammatory myopathy, IBM
Chronic reinnervation (large polyphasic MUPs)
Long-standing neuropathic process · Surviving axons have collateral sprouted extensively → giant MUPs · ALS signature
Small short-duration polyphasic MUPs + early recruitment
Myopathy · ± fibs (inflammatory, necrotizing, IBM) · NCS normal
Variable MUP morphology + normal NCS
NMJ disorder · Confirm with RNS · SFEMG most sensitive
The most important localizing distinction: Is the SNAP normal or abnormal?
Normal SNAP → lesion is at or proximal to the DRG (root lesion, anterior horn cell, UMN)
Abnormal SNAP → lesion is at or distal to the DRG (plexus, peripheral nerve, sensory ganglion)

NCS Parameters

What each parameter measures, what it reflects, and what changes it in pathology

🔵
CMAP — Compound Motor Action Potential
Recorded over muscle belly after motor nerve stimulation
Amplitude (mV)Sum of all simultaneously activated muscle fiber APs. Reflects the number of functional motor axons (and the muscle fibers they innervate). ↓ in axonal loss, conduction block, NMJ failure, severe myopathy.
Distal latency (ms)Time from distal stimulation site to CMAP onset. Includes terminal axon conduction + NMJ delay + muscle fiber activation. Reflects distal nerve segment function. ↑ in distal demyelination (e.g. CTS), ↑ slightly in axonal loss.
Conduction velocity (m/s)Calculated between two proximal stimulation points: distance ÷ latency difference. Reflects the fastest conducting (largest diameter, most myelinated) fibers. ↓↓ in demyelination; ↓ mildly in axonal loss (loss of fastest fibers).
Duration (ms)Total CMAP duration. ↑ (temporal dispersion) when different fibers arrive asynchronously due to non-uniform demyelination. Key indicator of acquired (non-hereditary) demyelination.
Area under curveIntegrates amplitude × duration. Can detect conduction block even when proximal CMAP amplitude looks normal but temporal dispersion is present. Area drop > amplitude drop = dispersion predominant.
Conduction block: ≥50% proximal-to-distal CMAP amplitude reduction (with distal CMAP >1 mV) in a segment not prone to compression, without excessive temporal dispersion. Implies reversible demyelination — potential for recovery.
🟢
SNAP — Sensory Nerve Action Potential
Recorded over sensory nerve after antidromic or orthodromic stimulation
Amplitude (μV)Reflects the number of functional sensory axons. Much smaller than CMAP (μV vs mV). ↓ in sensory axonal loss and distal to a DRG lesion. NORMAL in root lesions (pre-ganglionic) — the key localizing distinction.
Peak latency (ms)From stimulus to SNAP peak. Most commonly used for sensory latency measurements (e.g. CTS: median digit 2 peak latency).
Conduction velocity (m/s)Calculated from onset latency. Reflects fastest sensory fibers. ↓↓ in sensory demyelination; ↓ mildly with axonal loss.
Temperature effectCRITICAL: cold limb → ↑ amplitude, ↑ latency, ↓ CV (mimics pathology in reverse). Always warm limb to ≥32°C (surface) before testing.
Absent SNAP ≠ radiculopathy. Root lesions are pre-ganglionic → DRG cell body + peripheral axon intact → SNAP preserved. Absent SNAP in the same distribution = plexus or peripheral nerve (post-ganglionic). This is the single most important NCS localizing principle.
🟣
F-waves
Late motor responses — antidromic activation of anterior horn cells
MechanismMotor nerve supramaximal stimulation → antidromic AP travels to spinal cord → backfires a small % of anterior horn cells → orthodromic AP returns down motor axon to muscle. NOT a reflex.
MeasuresProximal motor conduction (roots, plexus, proximal nerve) — the segment inaccessible to direct NCS stimulation. Minimum F-wave latency used (fastest conducting motor unit).
AbnormalitiesAbsent or prolonged minimum latency (>ULN): proximal demyelination (early GBS — often the only abnormality initially), radiculopathy, plexopathy. Low persistence (<50%) also significant.
Normal valuesMedian ≤31 ms · Ulnar ≤32 ms · Tibial ≤56 ms · Peroneal ≤53 ms (height-dependent; see normal values table)
🟤
H-reflex
Electrical equivalent of the ankle jerk (monosynaptic S1 arc)
MechanismSubmaximal tibial nerve stimulation → Ia sensory fibers (not motor) → spinal cord → synapse on S1 motor neurons → response in soleus. Tests the complete S1 arc.
Clinical valueAbsent or asymmetric (>1.5 ms side-to-side difference): S1 radiculopathy, peripheral polyneuropathy (lost early in length-dependent neuropathies). More sensitive than EMG paraspinals for S1 root assessment.
LimitationOnly routinely recordable from soleus (S1). Absent in healthy elderly (>60y) in up to 20% — must compare sides. Cannot distinguish central from peripheral S1 arc lesion in isolation.
🔵
Repetitive Nerve Stimulation (RNS)
Tests neuromuscular junction — essential for suspected MG or LEMS
Slow RNS (2–3 Hz)Delivers 6–10 stimuli at 3 Hz. Decrement ≥10% in CMAP amplitude (4th–5th vs 1st) = postsynaptic NMJ failure. Sensitivity: ~70% in MG (higher in proximal muscles). Decrement corrects transiently after brief (10s) exercise (post-tetanic potentiation).
Fast RNS (50 Hz) or post-exerciseIncrement ≥100% in CMAP amplitude = presynaptic NMJ failure (LEMS). 10s of maximal voluntary contraction followed by immediate CMAP recording also shows dramatic increment (post-exercise facilitation) — safer than 50 Hz.
SFEMGSingle fiber EMG: measures jitter (variability in consecutive discharge intervals between two muscle fibers of same MU). Most sensitive NMJ test (~95% MG). Increased jitter ± blocking = NMJ transmission failure.
Key distinction: slow RNS decrement = postsynaptic (MG) · fast RNS/post-exercise increment = presynaptic (LEMS). Both can show decrement on slow RNS — the increment on fast RNS is specific to LEMS.

Axonal vs. Demyelinating

The fundamental NCS distinction — drives the entire differential diagnosis

AXONAL PATTERN
CMAP amplitude↓↓ (loss of axons)
SNAP amplitude↓↓ (loss of axons)
Conduction velocityNormal or mildly ↓ (loss of fast fibers)
Distal latencyNormal or mildly ↑
Temporal dispersionAbsent
Conduction blockAbsent
F-wavesNormal or mildly prolonged / absent (if severe)
Needle EMGFibs/PSWs + neurogenic MUPs
RecoverySlow (axon regrowth 1–3 mm/day)
Common causes: DM, alcohol, axonal GBS (AMAN), vasculitic, toxic, uremic, nutritional
DEMYELINATING PATTERN
CMAP amplitudeRelatively preserved (early); ↓ if conduction block
SNAP amplitudeRelatively preserved (early)
Conduction velocity↓↓ (<75% LLN = moderate; <60% = definite)
Distal latency↑↑ (>130% ULN)
Temporal dispersionPresent (duration >130% ULN)
Conduction blockPresent (acquired demyelination)
F-wavesAbsent or markedly prolonged (early sign)
Needle EMGOften relatively normal MUPs (axons intact); ↓ recruitment if conduction block
RecoveryFaster (remyelination weeks–months)
Common causes: GBS (AIDP), CIDP, CMT1, MMN, POEMS

Demyelination: Hereditary vs. Acquired

HEREDITARY (e.g. CMT1A)
Slowing patternUNIFORM across all nerves
CV15–30 m/s in ALL nerves
Conduction blockAbsent
Temporal dispersionAbsent (uniform slowing = all fibers slow equally)
F-wavesProlonged (uniform)
PatternAll nerves equally and symmetrically affected from childhood
MechanismDysmyelination — myelin never forms properly (PMP22 duplication in CMT1A)
ACQUIRED (e.g. CIDP, GBS)
Slowing patternNON-UNIFORM, multifocal
CVVaries between and within nerves
Conduction blockPresent (focal demyelination blocks impulse)
Temporal dispersionPresent (fibers with different degrees of demyelination)
F-wavesAbsent or very prolonged (disproportionate to CV)
PatternPatchy, asymmetric, any age of onset
MechanismImmune-mediated myelin destruction — previously normal myelin attacked
CV thresholds for demyelination (upper limb): <50 m/s motor = significant · <40 m/s = definite · <30 m/s = severe
Lower limb: <40 m/s motor = significant · <30 m/s = definite
These thresholds apply to acquired demyelination. In CMT1A, CV 15–30 m/s does NOT necessarily mean "severe" — the nerve is structurally dysmyelinated from birth.

Needle EMG

Spontaneous activity · MUP analysis · Recruitment patterns

Spontaneous Activity

Fibrillation Potentials (Fibs)
MorphologyBiphasic or triphasic, positive initial deflection. Amplitude 20–300 μV. Duration 1–5 ms. Regular firing rate 0.5–15 Hz. Sound"Rain on a tin roof" — regular, high-pitched click MechanismDenervated single muscle fiber — spontaneous oscillation of resting membrane potential. Also loss of terminal motor axon → fiber fires autonomously. SignificanceDenervation (neuropathic) · Inflammatory myopathy (PM, DM) · Necrotizing myopathy · IBM · Muscular dystrophy (some) TimingAppear 1–4 weeks after nerve injury: paraspinals 7–10 days → distal limb 2–4 weeks (related to Wallerian degeneration speed) Grading0 = none · 1+ = occasional (≥2 sites) · 2+ = moderate (most sites) · 3+ = many in all areas · 4+ = continuous
Positive Sharp Waves (PSWs)
MorphologySharp positive deflection followed by long negative tail. Same significance as fibs — they coexist almost always. Sound"Dull thud" — lower frequency than fibs SignificanceSame as fibrillations — denervation, inflammatory myopathy. Grade on same 0–4+ scale. NotePSWs appear slightly earlier after nerve injury than fibrillations (possibly from mechanically irritated fibers).
Fasciculation Potentials
MorphologyMUP-like (involves entire motor unit). Variable amplitude and duration. Irregular firing rate. SoundIrregular "pop" — the visible/palpable muscle twitch you see clinically MechanismSpontaneous discharge of an ENTIRE motor unit (vs. fibrillations = single fiber). Can originate anywhere along the motor unit — anterior horn cell, axon, NMJ. SignificanceComplex fasciculations (long duration, polyphasic, firing >3 Hz) + other abnormalities → ALS. Simple fasciculations alone → may be benign (benign fasciculation syndrome). Key ruleFasciculations ALONE are not diagnostic of ALS — need chronic reinnervation MUPs + fibs/PSWs in multiple regions.
Complex Repetitive Discharges (CRDs)
MorphologyStereotyped, high-frequency (5–100 Hz), complex waveform. Abrupt onset AND abrupt offset (like flipping a switch). Sound"Machine gun" or "motorcycle engine" — uniform, monotonous MechanismCircular discharge between adjacent muscle fibers via ephaptic transmission — one fiber re-excites the next in a loop, indefinitely sustaining until the loop breaks (abrupt offset). SignificanceChronic denervation OR chronic myopathy. Non-specific for cause but indicates chronicity. Common in radiculopathy, CIAP, chronic myopathies.
Myotonic Discharges
MorphologyRepetitive single muscle fiber APs with WAXING AND WANING amplitude AND frequency. This distinguishes from CRDs (which are stereotyped). Sound"Dive bomber" — characteristic rising and falling pitch and amplitude MechanismUnstable muscle fiber membrane → repetitive firing after mechanical (needle) or electrical stimulation. Caused by Na⁺ channel or Cl⁻ channel dysfunction. SignificanceMyotonic dystrophy (DM1, DM2) · Myotonia congenita (Becker, Thomsen) · Periodic paralysis (paramyotonia) · Sodium channelopathy · IBM (electrical myotonia without clinical grip myotonia)
Myokymic Discharges
MorphologyGroups (bursts) of MUPs firing repetitively at regular intervals, separated by silent periods. "Burst–pause–burst" pattern. Sound"Marching soldiers" — rhythmic grouped firing ClinicalVisible worm-like undulating muscle movement SignificanceFacial myokymia: MS (pontine demyelination), brainstem glioma, Lyme disease. Limb myokymia: radiation plexopathy (very characteristic), AIDP/CIDP. Generalised: Isaacs syndrome (neuromyotonia).
Neuromyotonic Discharges
MorphologyVery high frequency (150–300 Hz), irregular discharge of MUPs with decrementing amplitude. Continuous or semi-continuous. SoundHigh-pitched continuous sound, "pinging" quality SignificancePeripheral nerve hyperexcitability: Isaacs syndrome (CASPR2 antibodies), Morvan syndrome, thymoma, radiation neuropathy. Clinically: stiffness, cramps, hyperhidrosis, insomnia.

MUP Analysis

Amplitude (μV)
Normal: 100–2000 μV. Reflects fibers within ~500 μm of needle tip.
Increased (>2000 μV)
Chronic reinnervation: surviving axons sprout, adopt orphaned fibers → giant MUPs. ALS hallmark.
Decreased (<100 μV)
Myopathy (smaller fibers, fewer fibers/unit) · NMJ disease (variable amplitude MUPs)
Duration (ms)
Normal: 5–15 ms (muscle- and age-dependent). Reflects the territory of the motor unit.
Increased (>15 ms)
Neuropathic (reinnervation extends territory): chronic axonal neuropathy, ALS, old radiculopathy. Late components / satellite potentials = sprout-mediated reinnervation.
Decreased (<5 ms)
Myopathy (fewer fibers, smaller territory, loss of synchrony). Early recruitment at low force levels.
Polyphasia
Normal: <20% of MUPs have >4 phases. Each phase-crossing = a group of fibers arriving asynchronously.
Increased (>20%)
Neuropathic: reinnervating sprouts have slower conduction → late-arriving potentials → more phases.
Myopathic: loss of synchrony in small, disrupted MUs.
NMJ: variable blocking of individual fibers → polyphasia and instability.
Note
Polyphasia alone is non-specific. Interpret in context of amplitude and duration.

Recruitment Patterns

NEUROPATHIC (Reduced Recruitment)
Fewer motor units available (axonal loss or conduction block). Remaining MUs fire faster to compensate for lost units.
PatternDiscrete "picket fence" — single MUs visible at high effort
Firing rate>20–30 Hz for first unit (normally recruits second unit at ~10 Hz)
InterferenceReduced — can count individual MUPs at full effort
MUP size↑ amplitude + duration (chronic reinnervation)
MYOPATHIC (Early Recruitment)
Normal number of MUs, but each unit generates less force (fewer/smaller fibers per unit). Brain compensates by recruiting more MUs early and at low force levels.
PatternFull interference at low force — "crowded" small MUPs
Firing rateNormal onset rate (<10 Hz), early subsequent recruitment
InterferenceFull (many MUs) but amplitude small
MUP size↓ amplitude + duration (myopathic)
Recruitment ratio: firing rate of the first MU (Hz) ÷ total number of MUs firing. Normally <5. In neuropathy: high firing rate, few MUs → high ratio. In myopathy: normal firing rate, many MUs → low ratio.

Clinical Decoder

Report → localization workflow, and 6 common clinical scenarios with expected findings and traps

Reading the Report — Step-by-Step

1
Check SNAP first
If SNAP is NORMAL in a symptomatic distribution → the lesion is at or proximal to the DRG (root, anterior horn cell, CNS). If SNAP is ABNORMAL → the lesion is post-ganglionic (plexus, peripheral nerve, DRG itself in ganglionopathy).
2
Motor vs. Sensory vs. Sensorimotor
Pure motor NCS abnormality: anterior horn cell (ALS), pure motor neuropathy (MMNCB, GBS AMAN), radiculopathy with only motor root involvement. Pure sensory: ganglionopathy, early DSPN, paraneoplastic sensory neuropathy.
3
Axonal or Demyelinating?
Apply the criteria (see Axonal vs Demyelinating section). This is the single most important NCS distinction — it defines the differential diagnosis and the urgency of treatment (CIDP vs. axonal CIAP are managed very differently).
4
Distribution — Length-dependent or non-length-dependent?
Length-dependent (sock-glove, distal-first, symmetric) → metabolic/toxic/hereditary. Non-length-dependent (asymmetric, proximal and distal, multifocal) → vasculitic, immune-mediated, infiltrative, compressive. Focal/multifocal → entrapment, vasculitis, MMNCB.
5
Needle EMG — Denervation distribution
Map out which muscles show fibs/PSWs and chronic neuropathic MUPs. Does the pattern follow: a root? a nerve? a plexus cord? multiple roots (myelopathy)? proximal-distal (ALS)? Does it involve paraspinal muscles? Paraspinal fibs = root level involvement.
6
Synthesis — NCS + needle EMG + clinical = localization + mechanism
The electrodiagnostic conclusion must fit the clinical syndrome. A mismatch should prompt reconsideration of both. The study confirms or refutes the clinical hypothesis; it cannot replace it.

Common Clinical Scenarios

🦶 Foot drop in a diabetic patient
Peroneal neuropathy at fibular head
NCS: peroneal CMAP ↓ across fibular head, normal below · Focal slowing/block at fibular head · Tibial and sural normal or DSPN pattern · Needle EMG: fibs in tibialis anterior, EDB; spares short head biceps femoris (localizes to below knee) and paraspinals
L4/L5 Radiculopathy
NCS: SNAP NORMAL · Peroneal CMAP may be ↓ (severe) · Needle EMG: fibs/PSWs in L4/L5 distribution (TA, EDB, gluteus medius, tensor fascia lata) + PARASPINALS (key) · Short head biceps femoris SPARED (sciatic nerve, not root)
Key distinguisher: Paraspinal fibs = root level. Normal SNAPs favour root (proximal to DRG). Fibular head focal slowing = compressive neuropathy.
🖐️ Bilateral hand tingling — CTS vs. C6 radiculopathy vs. distal polyneuropathy
CTS (bilateral)
Prolonged median sensory peak latency digit 2 (>3.5 ms) + prolonged median motor DL (>4.4 ms) · Normal ulnar NCS · Compare median palm-wrist vs wrist-APB latency for localization · Normal APB if mild; fibs/neuropathic MUPs in APB if severe · Bilateral = bilateral CTS (common), not a systemic polyneuropathy
C6 Radiculopathy
Median SNAP NORMAL · CMAP may be ↓ · Fibs in C6 distribution: APB, pronator teres, FPL, ECRL + C6 paraspinals · H-reflex: C6/C7 FCR H-reflex may be absent/prolonged · Normal ulnar NCS
Distal Polyneuropathy
Sural and peroneal sensory abnormal FIRST, then upper limbs · All sensory amplitudes ↓ symmetrically · Mild CV slowing · Both median and ulnar SNAPs reduced equally · No focal slowing at wrist
💪 Proximal weakness with preserved reflexes
Myopathy
NCS: NORMAL · Needle EMG: small, short, polyphasic MUPs · Early recruitment · ± fibs (inflammatory) · Proximal > distal · Paraspinals often involved in inflammatory myopathy
LEMS
NCS: ↓ CMAP amplitude at rest (KEY) · Normal SNAP · RNS: ≥100% increment at 50 Hz or post-exercise · Needle EMG: variable amplitude MUPs
LEMS vs Myopathy trap: LEMS has LOW CMAP at rest — unusual finding that should prompt RNS. Myopathy has normal CMAP. Both have proximal weakness and normal/near-normal sensory NCS.
🧠 Ascending weakness + areflexia — GBS timing and findings
Week 1: F-waves absent or prolonged may be the ONLY finding. NCS may look nearly normal. Clinical diagnosis drives treatment — don't wait for classic NCS abnormalities.
Week 2–4: Distal latencies ↑↑, CV ↓, temporal dispersion, conduction block, sural sparing pattern (sural preserved when upper limb sensory nerves are more affected — characteristic of AIDP).
Week 4+: Fibs/PSWs appear if axonal loss component (AMAN/AMSAN variant) — implies slower recovery. Needle EMG abnormalities appear 2–4 weeks after disease onset.
Early GBS trap: Normal NCS in week 1 does NOT exclude GBS. F-waves may be the only window — examine them carefully. Absent F-waves in a clinically weak patient is a red flag.
🤔 Asymmetric weakness, fasciculations, no sensory loss — ALS criteria on EMG
Awaji criteria: Fasciculation potentials have the same diagnostic significance as fibrillations/PSWs (active denervation) in the context of ALS. Requires BOTH active denervation AND chronic reinnervation in ≥3 of 4 regions (bulbar, cervical, thoracic, lumbar).
Key NCS finding: Sensory NCS NORMAL (anterior horn cell disease — sensory neurons spared). CMAP amplitude ↓ in affected muscles. Normal CV (proportional to surviving axons).
Chronic reinnervation MUPs: Very large (>5 mV), long duration, polyphasic, with satellite potentials. Firing fast to compensate for lost motor units. These large MUPs + fasciculations + fibs = the ALS EMG signature.
Normal sensory NCS in a weak patient with fasciculations: Think ALS until proven otherwise. Abnormal sensory NCS should prompt looking for an alternative diagnosis (Kennedy disease/SBMA has sensory involvement; ALS does not).
🩺 Shoulder pain + wasting after illness (Parsonage-Turner / neuralgic amyotrophy)
NCS: SNAP abnormal in affected distribution (post-ganglionic plexus lesion — DRG included in territory). Motor CMAP ↓ in affected muscles. Important: pattern crosses root territories (e.g. both C5 and C6 muscles, or long thoracic + suprascapular + axillary nerve simultaneously).
Needle EMG: Fibs/PSWs and neurogenic MUPs in muscles of multiple root AND nerve territories — the hallmark. Unlike radiculopathy (single root territory). May show multiple mononeuropathy pattern. Paraspinals typically SPARED (plexus lesion, not root).
Plexopathy vs Radiculopathy: Abnormal SNAP + crosses root territories + spares paraspinals = plexopathy. Normal SNAP + single root territory + paraspinals involved = radiculopathy.
