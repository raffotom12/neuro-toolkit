Master Diagnostic Algorithm

Follow each decision node top-to-bottom — the SNAP localisation rule is the single most critical step

SNAP normal → lesion at or proximal to DRG → root / AHC / UMN
SNAP abnormal → lesion post-ganglionic → plexus / peripheral nerve / DRG

④B
Demyelinating — Uniform or Non-uniform?
Do all nerves show the same degree of slowing, or is it patchy / variable between nerves?
UNIFORM
All nerves equally slowed · No conduction block · No temporal dispersion
Hereditary neuropathy: CMT1A (PMP22 dup) · CMT1B (MPZ) · CMT4 subtypes · HNPP. CV typically 15–30 m/s throughout.
NON-UNIFORM
Patchy slowing · Conduction block · Temporal dispersion · Variable between nerves
Acquired (immune-mediated): CIDP · GBS / AIDP · MMN (motor-only + conduction block) · POEMS · Paraproteinaemic neuropathy

NCS Parameters

Anatomy of the CMAP and SNAP — what each parameter measures and what pathology changes it

● Motor NCS — CMAP
Amplitude = axon count Latency = distal segment CV = fastest fibres Duration = synchrony
● Sensory NCS — SNAP
Amplitude = sensory axon count Peak latency = distal segment CV = fastest sensory fibres Normal in ROOT lesions (pre-ganglionic)
● CV Measurement (Motor)
↓↓ CV = demyelination CV mildly ↓ = axonal (fastest fibres lost)

Parameter Reference

🔵
CMAP — Compound Motor Action Potential
Recorded over muscle belly after motor nerve stimulation
Amplitude (mV)Sum of all simultaneously activated muscle fibre APs. Reflects the number of functional motor axons. ↓ in axonal loss, conduction block, NMJ failure, severe myopathy.
Distal latency (ms)Time from distal stimulation to CMAP onset. Includes terminal axon conduction + NMJ delay + muscle activation. Reflects distal nerve segment function. ↑↑ in distal demyelination (e.g. CTS).
Conduction velocity (m/s)Distance ÷ latency difference between two stimulation points. Reflects the fastest conducting (largest-diameter, most-myelinated) fibres. ↓↓ in demyelination; ↓ mildly in axonal loss (loss of fast fibres).
Duration (ms)Total CMAP duration. ↑ (temporal dispersion) when fibres arrive asynchronously — non-uniform demyelination. Key indicator of acquired vs hereditary demyelination.
Conduction block: ≥50% proximal-to-distal CMAP amplitude reduction (with distal CMAP >1 mV), without excessive temporal dispersion. Implies reversible demyelination — potential for rapid recovery.
🟢
SNAP — Sensory Nerve Action Potential
Much smaller than CMAP (μV vs mV) — the most sensitive early marker of sensory axon loss
Amplitude (μV)Reflects number of functional sensory axons. ↓ post-ganglionic sensory loss. NORMAL in root lesions (pre-ganglionic) — the critical localising principle.
Peak latency (ms)From stimulus to SNAP peak. Most commonly used for sensory latency comparisons (e.g. CTS median digit 2 peak latency >3.5 ms).
TemperatureCold limb → ↑ amplitude, ↑ latency, ↓ CV (mimics pathology). Always warm limb to ≥32°C surface before testing.
Absent SNAP ≠ radiculopathy. Root lesions are pre-ganglionic → DRG + peripheral axon intact → SNAP preserved. Absent SNAP = lesion at or distal to DRG (plexus, peripheral nerve, ganglionopathy).
🟣
F-waves
Late motor response — antidromic activation of anterior horn cells — assesses proximal motor conduction
MechanismSupramaximal stimulation → antidromic AP to spinal cord → backfires a small % of anterior horn cells → orthodromic AP returns to muscle. NOT a reflex — no synapse involved.
Clinical useAssesses proximal motor conduction (roots, plexus, proximal nerve) — the segment inaccessible to direct NCS. Absent or prolonged F-waves = early GBS (often the ONLY early finding), radiculopathy, plexopathy.
Normal latenciesMedian ≤31 ms · Ulnar ≤32 ms · Tibial ≤56 ms · Peroneal ≤53 ms (height-dependent)
🔵
Repetitive Nerve Stimulation (RNS)
Tests neuromuscular junction — slow (3 Hz) vs fast (50 Hz) RNS distinguish postsynaptic from presynaptic NMJ failure
Slow RNS (3 Hz)Decrement ≥10% (4th vs 1st CMAP) = postsynaptic NMJ failure → Myasthenia Gravis. Partial correction after 10s exercise (post-tetanic potentiation). Sensitivity ~70% in MG.
Fast RNS (50 Hz) or post-exercise facilitationIncrement ≥100% in CMAP amplitude = presynaptic NMJ failure → LEMS. Check CMAP amplitude at rest first — LEMS has low resting CMAP; MG does not.
SFEMG (single fibre EMG): measures jitter between two fibres of same MU. Most sensitive NMJ test (~95% MG). Increased jitter ± blocking = NMJ transmission failure.

Spontaneous Activity

Fibrillation Potentials (Fibs)
MorphologyBiphasic/triphasic · 20–300 μV · 1–5 ms · Regular 0.5–15 Hz Sound"Rain on tin roof" — high-pitched, regular click MechanismDenervated single muscle fibre fires autonomously SignificanceDenervation · Inflammatory myopathy (PM/DM) · IBM · Necrotizing myopathy TimingParaspinals 7–10d · distal limb 2–4 wks after injury Grading0 = none · 1+ = occasional · 2+ = moderate · 3+ = many · 4+ = continuous
Positive Sharp Waves (PSWs)
MorphologySharp initial positive deflection → long negative tail. Same significance as fibs — almost always coexist. Sound"Dull thud" — lower frequency than fibs SignificanceSame as fibrillations — denervation, inflammatory myopathy. PSWs may precede fibs slightly after injury.
Fasciculation Potentials
MorphologyMUP-like — whole motor unit. Variable, irregular firing rate. SoundIrregular "pop" — the clinically visible muscle twitch SignificanceComplex fasciculations (long, polyphasic, >3 Hz) + denervation + reinnervation → ALS. Simple fasciculations alone may be benign.
Complex Repetitive Discharges (CRDs)
MorphologyStereotyped, high-frequency (5–100 Hz). Abrupt onset AND abrupt offset — like a switch. Sound"Machine gun" — uniform, monotonous SignificanceChronic denervation OR chronic myopathy (non-specific for cause — indicates chronicity).
Myotonic Discharges
MorphologyWaxing AND waning amplitude AND frequency — distinguishes from CRDs (stereotyped) and fasciculations (irregular). Sound"Dive bomber" — classic rising and falling pitch SignificanceMyotonic dystrophy DM1/DM2 · Myotonia congenita · Periodic paralysis · IBM (electrical myotonia without clinical grip myotonia)
Myokymic Discharges
MorphologyBursts of MUPs firing repetitively at regular intervals, separated by silence. "Burst–pause–burst". Sound"Marching soldiers" — rhythmic grouped firing SignificanceFacial myokymia: MS (pontine), brainstem glioma, Lyme. Limb: Radiation plexopathy (very characteristic), AIDP/CIDP. Generalised: Isaacs syndrome (neuromyotonia).
Neuromyotonic Discharges
MorphologyVery high frequency (150–300 Hz), decrementing amplitude, continuous or semi-continuous. SoundHigh-pitched continuous "pinging" SignificancePeripheral nerve hyperexcitability: Isaacs syndrome (CASPR2 Ab) · Morvan syndrome · Radiation neuropathy · Thymoma

MUP Analysis

Amplitude (μV)
Normal: 100–2000 μV. Reflects fibres within ~500 μm of needle tip.
↑ Amplitude (>2000 μV)
Chronic reinnervation: sprouting axons adopt orphaned fibres → giant MUPs. ALS hallmark.
↓ Amplitude (<100 μV)
Myopathy (smaller fibre territory) · NMJ disease (variable amplitude)
Duration (ms)
Normal: 5–15 ms (muscle- and age-dependent). Reflects territory of the motor unit.
↑ Duration (>15 ms)
Neuropathic reinnervation: sprouting extends territory → late components + satellite potentials.
↓ Duration (<5 ms)
Myopathy: fewer fibres, smaller territory, loss of synchrony.
Polyphasia
Normal: <20% of MUPs have >4 phases. Each phase-crossing = fibre group arriving asynchronously.
↑ Polyphasia (>20%)
Neuropathic: reinnervating sprouts have slower conduction → late phases. Myopathic: disrupted small MUs. NMJ: variable fibre blocking → waveform instability.
Recruitment ratio: firing rate of first MU (Hz) ÷ total number of MUs active. Normally <5. Neuropathic: high firing rate + few MUs → high ratio. Myopathic: normal firing rate + many MUs → low ratio.

Clinical Decoder

Report → localization workflow, and 6 common clinical scenarios with expected findings and traps

Reading the Report — Step-by-Step

1
Check SNAP first
If SNAP is NORMAL in a symptomatic distribution → the lesion is at or proximal to the DRG (root, anterior horn cell, CNS). If SNAP is ABNORMAL → the lesion is post-ganglionic (plexus, peripheral nerve, DRG itself in ganglionopathy).
2
Motor vs. Sensory vs. Sensorimotor
Pure motor NCS abnormality: anterior horn cell (ALS), pure motor neuropathy (MMNCB, GBS AMAN), radiculopathy with only motor root involvement. Pure sensory: ganglionopathy, early DSPN, paraneoplastic sensory neuropathy.
3
Axonal or Demyelinating?
Apply the criteria (see Axonal vs Demyelinating section). This is the single most important NCS distinction — it defines the differential diagnosis and the urgency of treatment (CIDP vs. axonal CIAP are managed very differently).
4
Distribution — Length-dependent or non-length-dependent?
Length-dependent (sock-glove, distal-first, symmetric) → metabolic/toxic/hereditary. Non-length-dependent (asymmetric, proximal and distal, multifocal) → vasculitic, immune-mediated, infiltrative, compressive. Focal/multifocal → entrapment, vasculitis, MMNCB.
5
Needle EMG — Denervation distribution
Map out which muscles show fibs/PSWs and chronic neuropathic MUPs. Does the pattern follow: a root? a nerve? a plexus cord? multiple roots (myelopathy)? proximal-distal (ALS)? Does it involve paraspinal muscles? Paraspinal fibs = root level involvement.
6
Synthesis — NCS + needle EMG + clinical = localization + mechanism
The electrodiagnostic conclusion must fit the clinical syndrome. A mismatch should prompt reconsideration of both. The study confirms or refutes the clinical hypothesis; it cannot replace it.

Common Clinical Scenarios

🦶 Foot drop in a diabetic patient
Peroneal neuropathy at fibular head
NCS: peroneal CMAP ↓ across fibular head, normal below · Focal slowing/block at fibular head · Tibial and sural normal or DSPN pattern · Needle EMG: fibs in tibialis anterior, EDB; spares short head biceps femoris (localizes to below knee) and paraspinals
L4/L5 Radiculopathy
NCS: SNAP NORMAL · Peroneal CMAP may be ↓ (severe) · Needle EMG: fibs/PSWs in L4/L5 distribution (TA, EDB, gluteus medius, tensor fascia lata) + PARASPINALS (key) · Short head biceps femoris SPARED (sciatic nerve, not root)
Key distinguisher: Paraspinal fibs = root level. Normal SNAPs favour root (proximal to DRG). Fibular head focal slowing = compressive neuropathy.
🖐️ Bilateral hand tingling — CTS vs. C6 radiculopathy vs. distal polyneuropathy
CTS (bilateral)
Prolonged median sensory peak latency digit 2 (>3.5 ms) + prolonged median motor DL (>4.4 ms) · Normal ulnar NCS · Compare median palm-wrist vs wrist-APB latency for localization · Normal APB if mild; fibs/neuropathic MUPs in APB if severe · Bilateral = bilateral CTS (common), not a systemic polyneuropathy
C6 Radiculopathy
Median SNAP NORMAL · CMAP may be ↓ · Fibs in C6 distribution: APB, pronator teres, FPL, ECRL + C6 paraspinals · H-reflex: C6/C7 FCR H-reflex may be absent/prolonged · Normal ulnar NCS
Distal Polyneuropathy
Sural and peroneal sensory abnormal FIRST, then upper limbs · All sensory amplitudes ↓ symmetrically · Mild CV slowing · Both median and ulnar SNAPs reduced equally · No focal slowing at wrist
💪 Proximal weakness with preserved reflexes
Myopathy
NCS: NORMAL · Needle EMG: small, short, polyphasic MUPs · Early recruitment · ± fibs (inflammatory) · Proximal > distal · Paraspinals often involved in inflammatory myopathy
LEMS
NCS: ↓ CMAP amplitude at rest (KEY) · Normal SNAP · RNS: ≥100% increment at 50 Hz or post-exercise · Needle EMG: variable amplitude MUPs
LEMS vs Myopathy trap: LEMS has LOW CMAP at rest — unusual finding that should prompt RNS. Myopathy has normal CMAP. Both have proximal weakness and normal/near-normal sensory NCS.
🧠 Ascending weakness + areflexia — GBS timing and findings
Week 1: F-waves absent or prolonged may be the ONLY finding. NCS may look nearly normal. Clinical diagnosis drives treatment — don't wait for classic NCS abnormalities.
Week 2–4: Distal latencies ↑↑, CV ↓, temporal dispersion, conduction block, sural sparing pattern (sural preserved when upper limb sensory nerves are more affected — characteristic of AIDP).
Week 4+: Fibs/PSWs appear if axonal loss component (AMAN/AMSAN variant) — implies slower recovery. Needle EMG abnormalities appear 2–4 weeks after disease onset.
Early GBS trap: Normal NCS in week 1 does NOT exclude GBS. F-waves may be the only window — examine them carefully. Absent F-waves in a clinically weak patient is a red flag.
🤔 Asymmetric weakness, fasciculations, no sensory loss — ALS criteria on EMG
Awaji criteria: Fasciculation potentials have the same diagnostic significance as fibrillations/PSWs (active denervation) in the context of ALS. Requires BOTH active denervation AND chronic reinnervation in ≥3 of 4 regions (bulbar, cervical, thoracic, lumbar).
Key NCS finding: Sensory NCS NORMAL (anterior horn cell disease — sensory neurons spared). CMAP amplitude ↓ in affected muscles. Normal CV (proportional to surviving axons).
Chronic reinnervation MUPs: Very large (>5 mV), long duration, polyphasic, with satellite potentials. Firing fast to compensate for lost motor units. These large MUPs + fasciculations + fibs = the ALS EMG signature.
Normal sensory NCS in a weak patient with fasciculations: Think ALS until proven otherwise. Abnormal sensory NCS should prompt looking for an alternative diagnosis (Kennedy disease/SBMA has sensory involvement; ALS does not).
🩺 Shoulder pain + wasting after illness (Parsonage-Turner / neuralgic amyotrophy)
NCS: SNAP abnormal in affected distribution (post-ganglionic plexus lesion — DRG included in territory). Motor CMAP ↓ in affected muscles. Important: pattern crosses root territories (e.g. both C5 and C6 muscles, or long thoracic + suprascapular + axillary nerve simultaneously).
Needle EMG: Fibs/PSWs and neurogenic MUPs in muscles of multiple root AND nerve territories — the hallmark. Unlike radiculopathy (single root territory). May show multiple mononeuropathy pattern. Paraspinals typically SPARED (plexus lesion, not root).
Plexopathy vs Radiculopathy: Abnormal SNAP + crosses root territories + spares paraspinals = plexopathy. Normal SNAP + single root territory + paraspinals involved = radiculopathy.
