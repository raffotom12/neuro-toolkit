🚨 Step 1 — Initial ED Triage
EXCLUDE FIRST
Life-Threatening Emergencies
Before considering vertigo as an isolated symptom, immediately exclude:
  • Vertebrobasilar / cerebellar stroke — HINTS, NIHSS, vascular risk factors
  • Cerebellar haemorrhage — sudden headache + vomiting + ataxia
  • Vertebral artery dissection — neck pain + vertigo after trauma/manipulation
  • Myocardial infarction / arrhythmia — vertigo + chest pain, presyncope, hypotension
  • Hypoglycaemia — altered GCS, diaphoresis, confusion

📋 Step 2 — Targeted History
Mnemonic: T – I – M – I – N – G
Timing (duration) · Initiation (how it starts) · Movement (positional triggers) · Intensity and associated symptoms · Number (recurrent episodes?) · General (past medical history)
KEY QUESTION #1
How long does each episode last?
DurationDiagnostic orientation
Seconds (< 1 min)BPPV, orthostatic hypotension
Minutes–hours (20 min – 12h)Ménière, vertebrobasilar TIA, vestibular migraine
Days (continuous > 24h)Vestibular neuritis, cerebellar stroke, labyrinthitis
Chronic (weeks–months)PPPD, psychogenic vertigo, ototoxicity, schwannoma
KEY QUESTION #2
Triggering factors?
  • Head/body position changes → BPPV (turning in bed, standing up, looking up)
  • Orthostasis → Orthostatic hypotension (measure BP supine/standing!)
  • Loud sounds / Valsalva manoeuvre → Tullio phenomenon, superior canal dehiscence
  • Spontaneous, no trigger → Vestibular neuritis, Ménière, stroke
  • Stress / anxiety / crowded environments → PPPD, vestibular migraine
KEY QUESTION #3
Associated symptoms?
  • Hearing loss + tinnitus + aural fullness → Ménière
  • Headache + photophobia + phonophobia → Vestibular migraine
  • Severe nausea/vomiting without focal deficits → Vestibular neuritis (but also cerebellar!)
  • Diplopia / dysarthria / dysphagia / limb weakness → STROKE — red flag!
  • Otalgia + otorrhoea + fever → Labyrinthitis (otogenic), mastoiditis
DON'T FORGET
Medication and medical history
  • Ototoxic medications: aminoglycosides, cisplatin, furosemide, salicylates
  • Medications causing dizziness: antiepileptics, antihypertensives, benzodiazepines, antidepressants
  • Vascular risk factors: hypertension, AF, diabetes, dyslipidaemia, smoking
  • Recent cranial/cervical trauma
  • Previous otological surgery
🔍 Step 3 — Physical Examination
GOLD STANDARD
HINTS Plus (for acute continuous vertigo)
Use in patients with acute vestibular syndrome (continuous vertigo > 24h + nystagmus + nausea/instability). Sensitivity >99% for stroke when performed correctly.
TestPeripheral (benign)Central (dangerous)
Head Impulse Positive (corrective saccade) ✓ Negative (normal VOR) ⚠️
Nystagmus Unidirectional, inhibited by fixation Bidirectional or purely vertical ⚠️
Test of Skew Absent Present (vertical skew deviation) ⚠️
Plus: new hearing loss Possible (labyrinthitis) Acute sensorineural HL → AICA stroke ⚠️
⚠️ 1 central sign = urgent neuroimaging
IF EPISODIC
Dix-Hallpike Manoeuvre (suspected BPPV)
Technique: patient seated → rotate head 45° toward the suspected side → rapid extension with head hanging 20° below the table. Hold 30-60 sec.
  • Positive: torsional-vertical nystagmus with latency (2-5 sec), crescendo-decrescendo, duration < 60 sec, fatigability → posterior canal BPPV
  • Bilaterally negative but BPPV suspected → try supine roll test for lateral canal (horizontal nystagmus)
  • Caution: nystagmus without latency, pure downbeat, persistent > 60 sec → suspect central cause
FULL EXAMINATION
Targeted neurological examination
  • Cranial nerves: particularly V, VII, VIII, IX-X (brainstem)
  • Cerebellar: finger-nose, heel-shin, dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesia
  • Gait: Romberg, Unterberger, tandem gait
  • Otoscopy: exclude otitis media, cholesteatoma, herpes oticus (Ramsay Hunt)
  • BP supine and standing: drop in SBP ≥ 20 or DBP ≥ 10 mmHg = orthostatic hypotension
  • Weber and Rinne (512 Hz tuning fork): conductive vs sensorineural hearing loss
⚖️ Step 4 — Central vs Peripheral
Fundamental question: is it peripheral or central?
This distinction guides everything: urgency, imaging, treatment, prognosis
PERIPHERAL
Features
  • Nystagmus unidirectional horizontal-torsional
  • Inhibited by visual fixation
  • Head impulse positive
  • Skew absent
  • Auditory symptoms possible
  • No focal neurological deficits
  • Nausea/vomiting severe (proportionate)
CENTRAL
Features
  • Nystagmus bidirectional / purely vertical / purely torsional
  • Not inhibited by fixation
  • Head impulse negative
  • Skew present
  • Focal neurological deficits
  • Severe ataxia disproportionate
  • Acute severe headache
CAUTION
Diagnostic pitfalls
  • Isolated cerebellar stroke can mimic vestibular neuritis (vertigo + nausea without obvious focal deficits). HINTS is essential!
  • AICA stroke can cause hearing loss + vertigo, resembling labyrinthitis
  • CT is poorly sensitive for posterior fossa stroke in the first 24-48h — if HINTS is central, request MRI-DWI even with negative CT
  • Vestibular migraine can have spontaneous nystagmus — but migraine history, triggers, and photophobia guide diagnosis
  • Atypical BPPV (downbeat nystagmus) → exclude craniocervical junction pathology
🎯 Step 5 — Pattern-based Diagnosis
A. Brief episodic vertigo (seconds)
BPPV
Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo
Most common cause of vertigo (~30-40%). Displaced otoconia in the semicircular canals.
  • Duration: 10-60 seconds per episode
  • Trigger: head position changes (turning in bed, standing up, hyperextended head)
  • Diagnosis: positive Dix-Hallpike (posterior canal ~80%) or supine roll test (lateral canal ~15%)
  • Course: self-limiting over weeks, but recurrence in ~50% within 5 years
B. Recurrent episodic vertigo (minutes–hours)
MÉNIÈRE
Ménière's Disease
  • Classic tetrad: rotatory vertigo (20 min – 12h) + fluctuating hearing loss + tinnitus + aural fullness
  • Diagnosis: AAO-HNS 2015 criteria: ≥ 2 vertigo episodes 20 min – 12h + documented sensorineural hearing loss + fluctuating aural symptoms
  • Audiometry: low-frequency hearing loss in early stages
  • Course: progressive hearing loss; attacks become less intense but hearing worsens
VESTIBULAR MIGRAINE
Vestibular Migraine
Second most common cause of episodic vertigo. Often underdiagnosed.
  • ICHD-3 criteria: ≥ 5 episodes of vestibular symptoms (5 min – 72h) + migraine history + ≥ 50% of episodes have at least 1 migrainous symptom (headache, photophobia, phonophobia, aura)
  • Caution: headache may be absent in 30% of episodes!
  • DD with Ménière: absence of documented hearing loss, migraine history, dietary/hormonal triggers
TIA
Vertebrobasilar TIA
  • Vertigo + at least one other neurological symptom (diplopia, dysarthria, ataxia, visual field defects)
  • Isolated vertigo is rarely TIA (< 1% of cases), but consider if multiple vascular risk factors present
  • If suspected → CT/MRI angiography posterior circulation + ABCD2 score
C. Acute continuous vertigo (days)
VESTIBULAR NEURITIS
Vestibular Neuritis
  • Cause: inflammation/HSV-1 reactivation in the vestibular ganglion (superior branch > inferior)
  • Clinical: severe acute rotatory vertigo, nausea/vomiting, instability, lasting days — without hearing loss
  • HINTS: positive HI + unidirectional nystagmus + no skew = peripheral
  • Course: acute phase 1-3 days, progressive improvement over 1-6 weeks via central vestibular compensation
STROKE
Cerebellar / brainstem stroke
  • PICA / AICA / SCA: different territories, variable presentations
  • Central HINTS even with negative CT → urgent MRI-DWI
  • AICA stroke: vertigo + acute hearing loss + facial deficit → do not confuse with labyrinthitis
  • Isolated cerebellar stroke: vertigo + severe ataxia (unable to walk) + vomiting → do not confuse with neuritis
D. Chronic vertigo (weeks–months)
PPPD
Persistent Postural-Perceptual Dizziness
  • Diagnosis of exclusion + Bárány Society 2017 criteria
  • Dizziness/instability for ≥ 3 months, present most days
  • Worsened by: upright posture, complex visual stimuli, active/passive head movements
  • Often precipitated by an acute vestibular event (e.g. BPPV, neuritis) + failed compensation + anxiety
  • Normal neurological and vestibular examination
💊 Step 6 — Treatment
BPPV
Repositioning manoeuvres
  • Posterior canal: Epley manoeuvre (efficacy ~80% in 1-2 sessions)
  • Lateral canal (geotropic): Lempert/BBQ manoeuvre (360° rotation toward healthy side)
  • Lateral canal (apogeotropic): Gufoni manoeuvre
  • Do not prescribe vestibular suppressants for BPPV — they delay compensation
  • Recurrence → repeat manoeuvre; refractory BPPV → ENT assessment (canal plugging?)
VESTIBULAR NEURITIS
Acute treatment + rehabilitation
PhaseTreatment
Acute (24-72h) Vestibular suppressants: dimenhydrinate 50 mg IV/IM or metoclopramide 10 mg for nausea/vomiting.
Diazepam 5-10 mg only if symptoms very severe, for max 48-72h.
Subacute (> 72h) STOP vestibular suppressants (they slow compensation!).
Start vestibular rehabilitation as soon as possible.
Corticosteroids Controversial evidence. If used: methylprednisolone 100 mg → taper over 3 weeks. Within 72h of onset.
⚠️ Antivirals NOT indicated in vestibular neuritis

VESTIBULAR MIGRAINE
Prophylaxis + Acute treatment
  • Prophylaxis (as for migraine): propranolol 40-160 mg, amitriptyline 10-50 mg, topiramate 50-100 mg, flunarizine 5-10 mg, candesartan 8-16 mg, anti-CGRP antibodies
  • Acute: triptans (if headache present), NSAIDs, antiemetics
  • Lifestyle: sleep hygiene, regular exercise, avoid triggers
  • Vestibular rehabilitation: useful as adjunct
PPPD
Multimodal approach
  • Pharmacotherapy: SSRI/SNRI (sertraline 50-200 mg, venlafaxine 75-225 mg) — slow titration
  • Vestibular rehabilitation: personalised programme, gradual exposure
  • CBT: cognitive-behavioural therapy for anxiety component
  • Psychoeducation: reassure the patient, explain the mechanism (it's not "all in their head")
STROKE
Vertebrobasilar stroke management
  • IV thrombolysis: alteplase 0.9 mg/kg (within 4.5h) if indicated
  • Thrombectomy: basilar occlusion → extended window (up to 24h in experienced centres)
  • Cerebellar haemorrhage > 3 cm or with hydrocephalus → neurosurgical consult for evacuation
  • Admission to Stroke Unit, monitoring, secondary prevention
🚩 Step 7 — Red Flags & When to Escalate
RED FLAGS — URGENT NEUROIMAGING
Indications for urgent MRI-DWI / CT
  • Central HINTS (even a single component)
  • Purely vertical nystagmus (downbeat/upbeat) or purely torsional
  • Associated focal neurological deficits (diplopia, dysarthria, limb weakness, hemianopia)
  • Severe ataxia (unable to walk) disproportionate to vertigo
  • Severe acute headache of new onset ("worst of life")
  • Acute sensorineural hearing loss + vertigo (suspected AICA)
  • Altered consciousness
  • Multiple vascular risk factors + acute vertigo even if HINTS appears peripheral → low threshold for imaging
ESCALATE TO SENIOR
When to escalate on call
  • Any suspicion of central aetiology — always
  • Discordant or unclear HINTS → always better to get a second opinion
  • Patient with vertigo + AF not anticoagulated or with sub-therapeutic INR
  • Acute deterioration or new neurological symptoms during observation
  • Need for thrombolysis / thrombectomy
  • Cerebellar haemorrhage or acute hydrocephalus
  • Diagnostic uncertainty: it is always better to call than not to call
SAFE DISCHARGE
When discharge from ED is appropriate
  • Complete and reliable peripheral HINTS
  • BPPV diagnosed and treated with manoeuvre → nystagmus resolved
  • No red flags and no neurological deficits
  • Patient mobilising safely
  • Arrange follow-up: ENT/vestibology clinic within 1-2 weeks
  • Written discharge instructions: when to return to ED (new neurological symptoms, deterioration)
