💡
A complex neurological disorder with stereotyped phases: prodrome (hours–days before: yawning, food cravings, mood change, photophobia) → aura (if present, 5–60 min) → headache phase (4–72 h) → postdrome ("migraine hangover": fatigue, cognitive fog). Pathophysiology: cortical spreading depression underlies aura; trigeminovascular activation and CGRP release drive the headache. Hypothalamus involved in prodrome. Strong genetic component.
🚨
Red flags — exclude secondary headache: Thunderclap onset ("worst headache of life"), new headache after age 50, progressive course, nocturnal awakening, postural component, fever / meningismus, neurological deficits beyond typical aura, papilledema, immunosuppression / HIV, recent head trauma, headache with Valsalva (cough, exertion).
ICHD-3 Diagnostic Criteria
1.1 — Migraine Without Aura
A
≥ 5 attacks fulfilling criteria B–D
B
Duration 4–72 hours when untreated or unsuccessfully treated
C
≥ 2 of the following headache characteristics:
  • Unilateral location
  • Pulsating quality
  • Moderate or severe intensity
  • Aggravation by or causing avoidance of routine physical activity (walking, climbing stairs)
D
During headache, ≥ 1 of the following:
  • Nausea and/or vomiting
  • Photophobia and phonophobia (both required)
E
Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis
1.2 — Migraine With Aura
A
≥ 2 attacks fulfilling criteria B–C
B
≥ 1 fully reversible aura symptom:
  • Visual (most common — fortification spectra, scotoma)
  • Sensory (unilateral paraesthesiae / hypoaesthesia)
  • Speech or language (aphasia / dysarthria)
  • Motor (hemiplegic migraine — separate subtype 1.2.3)
  • Brainstem (diplopia, vertigo, tinnitus, ataxia — 1.2.2)
  • Retinal (monocular visual symptoms — 1.2.4)
C
≥ 3 of the following six characteristics:
  • At least one aura symptom spreads gradually over ≥ 5 minutes
  • Two or more aura symptoms occur in succession
  • Each individual aura symptom lasts 5–60 minutes
  • At least one aura symptom is unilateral
  • At least one aura symptom is positive (scintillations, pins and needles)
  • Headache accompanies or follows aura within 60 minutes
D
Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis — exclude TIA (MRI DWI/PWI if clinically uncertain)
⏱️
1.3 Chronic Migraine: Headache on ≥ 15 days/month for >3 months, with ≥ 8 days/month fulfilling migraine criteria (with or without acute medication use). Exclude medication overuse headache (MOH) before diagnosing.

Acute / Abortive Treatment — IHS 2024 & SISC/IHS 2025
✅
Stratified care: Treat based on attack severity. For mild–moderate attacks: NSAIDs ± antiemetic. For moderate–severe or failed NSAIDs: triptan or gepant. Always take acute medication early in the attack. Combine NSAID + triptan for superior efficacy in severe attacks.

⚠️
Medication Overuse Headache (MOH) risk: Triptans / ergots >10 days/month; simple analgesics >15 days/month → chronic daily headache. Withdrawal + bridge therapy (short corticosteroid course) + initiate prophylaxis. Gepants appear to carry lower MOH risk (ongoing study).
Prophylaxis — IHS Global Practice Recommendations 2024 & SISC/IHS 2025
📋
Indications for preventive treatment: ≥4 migraine days/month OR significantly disabling attacks (MIDAS ≥ Grade III) OR analgesic overuse OR patient preference. Also consider for special forms (hemiplegic, basilar, prolonged aura). Reassess after 3–6 months; aim for ≥50% reduction in headache frequency.

Nutraceuticals — Tepper et al. 2025 (Curr Pain Headache Rep) & AAN Evidence Review
ℹ️
Safe profile, minimal drug interactions. Useful as adjuncts, in patients preferring non-pharmacological approaches, or in those with contraindications to standard drugs. Minimum 3-month trial before assessing efficacy. Can be combined with pharmacological preventives.

Magnesium Level B

400–600 mg/day (oral)
1–2 g IV (acute aura)
NMDA receptor antagonism, cortical spreading depression (CSD) threshold ↑, CGRP modulation, platelet aggregation ↓. Deficiency documented in migraineurs. IV MgSO₄ especially effective for migraine with aura in ED.
Prefer citrate or glycinate (better GI tolerability vs. oxide). SE: diarrhea at high doses. Check serum Mg²⁺ if possible. Takes 4–6 weeks to take effect.

Riboflavin (B2) Level B

400 mg/day (adults)
200 mg/day (paediatric)
Mitochondrial electron transport chain (Complex I and II). Hypothesis: reduced mitochondrial reserve lowers threshold for CSD. High doses needed to saturate flavoenzymes. Paediatric evidence positive in multiple studies.
Excellent tolerability. SE: harmless orange-yellow urine discoloration (warn patient). No serious adverse events. Onset: 4–8 weeks.

CoQ10 (Ubiquinone) Level C

300 mg/day (3 × 100 mg) or 100–400 mg/day
Mitochondrial Complex I–III electron transport. RCT (Sandor 2005): 47.6% responder rate (≥50% reduction) vs. 14.4% placebo, NNT ≈ 3. Deficiency found in ~33% of paediatric migraineurs.
Take with a fatty meal (fat-soluble). Ubiquinol form has higher bioavailability. SE: minimal (mild GI). Trial 3 months minimum.

Melatonin Level C

3 mg at bedtime
One RCT: non-inferior to amitriptyline 25 mg with better tolerability. CGRP release modulation, antinociceptive, antioxidant. Low certainty of evidence overall (Tepper 2025). Best for patients with disrupted sleep.
SE: morning drowsiness (low dose minimises this). Also used at higher doses (10 mg) for cluster headache prophylaxis.

Feverfew Level C

50–150 mg/day (standardised dried leaf)
Parthenolide (active constituent): inhibits platelet serotonin release, prostaglandin synthesis, NF-κB. Evidence inconsistent across trials; use standardised extract to ensure parthenolide content ≥0.2%.
SE: mouth ulcers, GI upset. "Post-feverfew syndrome" on abrupt discontinuation (rebound headache, anxiety). Avoid in pregnancy. Not recommended by Tepper 2025 due to safety uncertainty.

Vitamin D Emerging

1000–4000 IU/day (correct deficiency)
Deficiency associated with migraine chronification. Supplementation may reduce attack frequency in deficient patients. Modulates neuroinflammation and serotonin synthesis. Not yet in main IHS/AAN guidelines.
Check 25-OH-D3 level first. Target: ≥30 ng/mL (≥75 nmol/L). Deficiency correction is always warranted regardless of migraine.

Chronic form
💡
Most prevalent primary headache disorder — enormous socioeconomic burden. Pathophysiology: episodic TTH driven by peripheral sensitisation of pericranial myofascial nociceptors; chronic TTH involves central sensitisation and impaired supraspinal pain modulation (descending pain inhibition failure). Strong association with stress, poor sleep, anxiety, and depression. Pericranial muscle tenderness (Total Tenderness Score) is a key objective finding.

2.3 — Chronic TTH
🕐
Headache on ≥ 15 days/month for >3 months (average >180 days/year). Same pain characteristics as episodic TTH but may have mild nausea (no vomiting). Evolves from frequent episodic form in most patients. Always exclude Medication Overuse Headache (analgesics >15 days/month) — may mimic or co-exist.

🔍
Clinical pearl: Pericranial tenderness (temporal, masseter, suboccipital muscles) is a key associated finding and is higher in chronic TTH. EMG biofeedback can reduce this tenderness. Key differentiator vs migraine: TTH features ≤1 of photophobia/phonophobia (not both), no nausea, not aggravated by activity, bilateral pressing quality.
Acute Treatment — EFNS Guidelines & VA/DoD 2023
⚠️
Limit analgesic use to ≤ 2 days/week to prevent medication overuse headache. For infrequent episodic TTH, over-the-counter analgesics are appropriate first-line therapy. Triptans, opioids, and barbiturate-containing combination analgesics should be avoided.

Prophylaxis — EFNS / VA–DoD 2023
📋
Indications: Frequent episodic TTH (≥2 attacks/week, significant impairment) or chronic TTH (≥15 days/month). Goal: ≥50% reduction in headache frequency. Duration: minimum 6 months. Always combine with non-pharmacological approaches.

DrugDoseEv.Notes

💀
"Suicide headache" — the most severe primary headache disorder. Trigeminal autonomic cephalalgia (TAC). Pathophysiology: Hypothalamic activation (posterior hypothalamus — the "pacemaker" for cluster periods) → trigeminovascular and parasympathetic activation (via sphenopalatine ganglion) → CGRP and VIP release. Circadian and circannual periodicity. Often nocturnal; REM-sleep related onset. Strong association with smoking (≥80% smokers in some series) and alcohol as trigger during active period.

A
≥ 5 attacks fulfilling criteria B–D
B
Severe or very severe unilateral orbital, supraorbital and/or temporal pain lasting 15–180 minutes when untreated

  • ≥ 1 of the following symptoms or signs, ipsilateral to the headache:

    • Nasal congestion and/or rhinorrhea

Acute / Abortive Treatment — EAN 2023 (European J Neurology)
⚡
Key principle: Attack duration is short (15–180 min) — only fast-acting treatments work. Oral medications are too slow. At diagnosis, prescribe both O₂ setup (non-rebreather mask + cylinder) and SC sumatriptan simultaneously. Start prophylaxis on the same day as acute treatment.
